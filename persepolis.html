Persepolis: Inside the Lost Ceremonial Capital of the Persian Empire

The Gate of All Nations, the Apadana, the Immortals, the Faravahar, and the night Alexander the Great burned it all to the ground — a complete field guide to the Achaemenid Empire's greatest stage.

13 MIN READ · UPDATED 2026 · FARS, IRAN

Persepolis was never really a capital in the everyday sense — no one lived there year-round, no markets ran through its halls. It was built as a stage: a ceremonial complex where, once a year, delegations from every corner of the Achaemenid Empire arrived to bring tribute to the King of Kings during Nowruz, the Persian New Year.

Construction began under Darius I around 518 BCE and continued for nearly 150 years under his successors, Xerxes I and Artaxerxes I. What survives today — a terrace of broken columns, carved staircases, and gates guarded by stone bulls — is a fraction of what once stood. But it's enough to understand why, over two thousand years later, this place still stops people mid-sentence.

01 / 08

The Gate of All Nations

📍 Persepolis, Fars Province, Iran  ·  Built under Xerxes I, c. 486 BCE

Every delegation entering Persepolis passed through this single gate — a monumental hall flanked by colossal bull-man figures carved in the Assyrian tradition, part guardian and part warning. A trilingual inscription cut into the stone, in Old Persian, Elamite, and Babylonian, records Xerxes' own words: that he built it by the will of Ahura Mazda.

Ambassadors, soldiers, and nobles from as far as India and Ethiopia all walked through this exact threshold — the only formal entrance to the entire terrace.

BUILT BY
Xerxes I
GUARDIANS
Colossal lamassu (bull-man) figures
INSCRIPTION
Trilingual — Old Persian, Elamite, Babylonian
02 / 08

The Apadana Palace

📍 Persepolis Terrace  ·  Begun by Darius I, completed by Xerxes I

The largest and most important structure on the terrace — a columned audience hall built to hold thousands of people at once for the king's Nowruz reception. Of its original 72 columns, each roughly 24 meters tall, only a little over a dozen are still standing today.

The staircases leading up to the Apadana carry its most famous feature: a continuous relief of 23 subject nations, each shown in their own dress, bringing their own regional tribute — horses, textiles, gold, and exotic animals — to the throne.

ORIGINAL COLUMNS
72, roughly 24 m tall
FUNCTION
Royal audience hall for Nowruz
FAMOUS FOR
Reliefs of 23 tribute-bearing nations
03 / 08

The Immortals

📍 Apadana & Tripylon reliefs

Herodotus described them as the elite infantry of the Achaemenid army — a force of exactly 10,000 men, whose numbers never seemed to change. Historians believe this was by design: the moment a soldier fell, died, or fell ill, a fully trained replacement stepped in immediately, keeping the unit permanently at full strength.

Row after row of them march across the Persepolis reliefs, spears and shields in hand, depicted with the same disciplined repetition that likely defined them on the battlefield.

UNIT SIZE
10,000 soldiers, kept constant
ROLE
Elite royal infantry guard
SOURCE
Described by Herodotus
04 / 08

The Tachara — Palace of Darius

📍 Persepolis Terrace  ·  Built by Darius I

Unlike the Apadana, built for crowds, the Tachara was personal — Darius I's private residence, constructed from dark grey stone polished smooth enough to reflect like a mirror. Its doorways are carved with reliefs of the king himself, attended by servants carrying royal furniture.

An inscription on the building states plainly that this palace was built for Darius specifically, and later kings who used it added their own names beside his rather than replacing them.

BUILT BY
Darius I
FUNCTION
Private royal residence
MATERIAL
Polished dark grey stone
05 / 08

The Faravahar

📍 Carved above several doorways across the terrace

One of the most recognizable symbols to come out of ancient Persia: a winged disc with a human figure at its center, arm outstretched. What it actually meant to the Achaemenids is still debated among historians — theories range from a representation of the fravashi (a guardian spirit) to a symbol of khvarenah, or divine royal glory.

It was carved prominently at Persepolis long before it became associated with Zoroastrianism in later centuries, and it remains one of the most widely recognized emblems of pre-Islamic Persian identity today.

FORM
Winged disc with human figure
MEANING
Debated — guardian spirit or royal glory
LATER USE
Associated with Zoroastrianism
06 / 08

Rhytons and Royal Tribute

📍 Depicted throughout the Apadana staircase reliefs

Among the most detailed carvings at Persepolis are the tribute-bearers shown holding rhytons — ceremonial drinking vessels carved with animal heads at the base, often lions, bulls, or griffins. Delegations from Lydia and Armenia are specifically recorded bringing vessels like these as gifts to the king.

Actual rhytons recovered from Achaemenid sites, made of gold and silver, confirm just how accurate these stone reliefs were — the carvers were clearly working from objects they had seen firsthand.

MATERIAL
Gold, silver (originals); carved stone (reliefs)
FORM
Animal-headed drinking vessels
PURPOSE
Ceremonial tribute gifts
07 / 08

The Burning of Persepolis

📍 330 BCE

In 330 BCE, after defeating the last Achaemenid king, Alexander the Great's forces set fire to Persepolis. Ancient sources disagree on whether it was calculated policy, a drunken accident at a banquet, or deliberate revenge for the Persian burning of Athens 150 years earlier — historians still debate which explanation is closest to the truth.

Whatever the reason, the fire brought an end to Achaemenid Persepolis for good. Charring is still visible on some of the stone columns and doorframes on-site today, more than two thousand years later.

DATE
330 BCE
RESPONSIBLE
Forces of Alexander the Great
DEBATED CAUSE
Policy, accident, or revenge for Athens
08 / 08

Visiting Persepolis Today

📍 25 km northeast of Marvdasht, Fars Province, Iran

Persepolis has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979, and remains one of the best-preserved windows into Achaemenid architecture anywhere in the world. It sits roughly 60 kilometers from Shiraz, making it an easy day trip, and is often paired with a visit to the nearby royal tombs at Naqsh-e Rustam.

Spring and autumn are the most comfortable seasons to visit — summer heat on the open terrace can be intense, with little natural shade across most of the site.

STATUS
UNESCO World Heritage Site (1979)
NEAREST CITY
Shiraz (~60 km)
BEST SEASON
March – May, Sept – Nov
