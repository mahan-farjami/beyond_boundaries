Pahrast Sofla: Inside One of Iran's Smallest, Least-Documented Villages

A 218-person village on the edge of the Larestan desert, wrapped in date palms, speaking a language older than modern Persian — and barely mentioned anywhere in English until now.

10 MIN READ · UPDATED 2026 · FARS, IRAN

Most places we cover here have guidebooks, documentaries, or at least a solid Wikipedia page in a dozen languages. Pahrast Sofla has almost none of that. It's a village of 218 people, tucked into the Baladeh rural district of the Biram district in Iran's Larestan county — the kind of place that shows up on a census spreadsheet and almost nowhere else.

That's exactly why it's here. Not every extraordinary place on Earth is famous. Some of them are just quietly, stubbornly still standing — a scatter of homes and palm groves against a hard desert landscape, doing what they've done for generations. This is our attempt at the first real English-language look at Pahrast Sofla and the world around it.

01 / 03

Where Pahrast Sofla Actually Is

📍 Baladeh Rural District, Biram District, Larestan County, Fars Province, Iran

Pahrast Sofla sits in the far south of Fars province, in one of Iran's most arid corners — a region where mountains flatten out into desert basin, and where any patch of green usually means people found a way to bring water to it. According to Iran's 2016 national census, the village is home to 218 people across 68 households.

It shares its immediate area with a near-twin village, Pahrast Olya ("Upper Pahrast"), and sits within reach of a scattering of other small settlements — Jowzqan, Lavarestan, Kereshki — that make up the wider Biram district.

POPULATION
218 people, 68 households (2016 census)
DISTRICT
Baladeh Rural District, Biram
PROVINCE
Fars, Iran

Life at the Edge of the Desert

📍 Larestan region, southern Fars

The wider Biram district reports an average annual rainfall under 200 millimeters — hot, dry, and unforgiving for most of the year. What makes villages like Pahrast Sofla possible at all are the date palm groves that ring them, dense enough in places to look almost like a forest against the pale desert floor.

Life here runs on a different rhythm than a city — homes clustered close together for shade and shelter, streets that go quiet by early evening, and a landscape that looks completely different at sunrise, midday, and dusk, when the heat finally breaks and the whole village seems to exhale.

CLIMATE
Hot & arid, under 200mm rain/year
LANDSCAPE
Desert plain ringed by date palm groves
ECONOMY
Date palm cultivation-based, like the wider region

A Language Older Than Modern Persian

One of the most striking things about this corner of Fars has nothing to do with architecture — it's what you'd hear if you stood on the street. Larestan, along with the nearby counties of Khonj, Gerash, and Evaz, is home to Larestani, also known locally as Achomi or Khodmooni — a language descended directly from Middle Persian (Pahlavi), the language spoken in Iran before modern Persian took shape.

It isn't a dialect of Persian so much as a parallel branch — different enough in grammar and vocabulary that linguists classify it as its own Southwestern Iranian language. It's still spoken daily across this region and among diaspora communities from southern Iran living around the Persian Gulf, including in the UAE, where much of the historical ruling and merchant class traces its roots back to Larestan.

LANGUAGE
Larestani (Achomi / Khodmooni)
ORIGIN
Descended from Middle Persian (Pahlavi)
ALSO SPOKEN
Persian Gulf diaspora communities, incl. UAE

Biram — The City of Imamzadehs Next Door

The district's namesake town, Biram, lies close by and gives the whole area its administrative center — home to roughly 7,300 people, promoted from village to town status in 1995. It carries a well-known local nickname: "the City of Imamzadehs," and is sometimes called the Dar al-Awliya, or "abode of saints," of Larestan, largely because of the revered shrine of Seyed Afifuddin, known locally as Shah Zandu.

Where the name "Biram" itself comes from is genuinely disputed. One local account traces it to the Turkish word bayram, meaning "festival," said to mark a historical military commander's victory celebration in the area. Another ties it to the Islamic-era arrival of forces under Seyed Afifuddin in the 9th century CE, with local tradition holding that the region's original inhabitants were Zoroastrian before that.

BIRAM TOWN
~7,300 people, 2,125 households (2016)
NICKNAME
"City of Imamzadehs"
NAME ORIGIN
Disputed — Turkish "bayram" or early Islamic-era history

Visiting This Corner of Fars

📍 Via Biram, Larestan County

Pahrast Sofla isn't set up for tourism, and that's part of what makes it worth mentioning at all — this is a working village, not a destination built for visitors. The practical route in is through Biram, the district's main town, which sits roughly 135 kilometers from the center of Larestan County and about 360 kilometers from Shiraz, the provincial capital.

Given the climate, the cooler months — late autumn through early spring — are by far the more comfortable time to be out on these desert roads, when the palm groves and the wide, empty distances between villages are easiest to actually enjoy.

NEAREST TOWN
Biram (district center)
DISTANCE TO SHIRAZ
~360 km
BEST SEASON
Late autumn to early spring
