The 7 Wonders of the Ancient World (And What's Actually Left of Them)

Only one still stands. The other six exist only in ancient descriptions, ruins, and educated guesses — here's what each one actually was, and what happened to it.

13 MIN READ·UPDATED 2026·ACROSS 3 CONTINENTS

The phrase "Seven Wonders of the World" gets thrown around constantly, but almost nobody can actually name all seven — and there's a good reason for that. The original list wasn't voted on by any government or committee. It comes down to us largely through a 2nd-century-BCE poem by Antipater of Sidon, essentially one well-traveled Greek writer's personal shortlist of the most impressive structures he knew of around the Mediterranean and Near East.

What makes the list genuinely remarkable today isn't just the scale of these structures — it's that six of the seven are gone. Wars, earthquakes, fires, and roughly 2,000+ years of time have reduced most of them to descriptions in old texts and scattered fragments in museums. Only one has survived intact into the present day, and it's also, by far, the oldest.

01 / 07 STILL STANDING

The Great Pyramid of Giza

📍 29.9792° N, 31.1342° E  ·  Giza, Egypt

Built as a tomb for the Pharaoh Khufu around 2560 BCE, the Great Pyramid held the record for tallest man-made structure on Earth for roughly 3,800 years — a record no other building broke until the 14th century. It's built from an estimated 2.3 million stone blocks, some weighing up to 80 tons, without any of the modern lifting equipment that would make the job straightforward even today.

It's the only one of the original seven wonders that still exists in anything close to its original form, and it's also the oldest one on the list by nearly 2,000 years — meaning it was already ancient history to the Greek and Roman writers who first compiled this list.

BUILT
c. 2560 BCE
ORIGINAL HEIGHT
146.6 m
STATUS TODAY
Fully intact, UNESCO site
02 / 07 LOST / DISPUTED

The Hanging Gardens of Babylon

📍 Near modern Hillah, Iraq (disputed)  ·  c. 600 BCE

Ancient accounts describe a multi-tiered garden of trees, vines, and flowering plants, built to resemble a green mountain rising above the flat Mesopotamian plain — supposedly constructed by King Nebuchadnezzar II for his homesick wife, who missed the mountains of her homeland. Irrigation would have required an advanced system to lift water from the Euphrates up through each terrace.

Here's the twist: despite being one of the most famous wonders on the list, archaeologists have never definitively located the gardens, and no Babylonian text from Nebuchadnezzar's own era mentions them at all — only later Greek writers describe them. One leading theory suggests the writers actually described a real garden, but at Nineveh, in a different empire entirely, and the location got confused over centuries of retelling.

03 / 07 DESTROYED

The Statue of Zeus at Olympia

📍 Olympia, Greece  ·  c. 435 BCE

Standing roughly 12 meters tall inside the Temple of Zeus — home of the original Olympic Games — this seated statue was carved by the sculptor Phidias from ivory and gold plates layered over a wooden frame, a technique called chryselephantine sculpture. Ancient visitors described the statue as so massive that if Zeus had stood up, he would have put his head through the temple roof.

The statue survived for roughly 800 years before being moved to Constantinople, where it was destroyed in a fire in the 5th century CE. Nothing of the original survives — everything known about its appearance comes from ancient coins, written descriptions, and smaller Roman-era copies.

SCULPTOR
Phidias
HEIGHT
~12 m seated
STATUS TODAY
Destroyed, 5th century CE
04 / 07 DESTROYED

The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus

📍 Near modern Selçuk, Turkey  ·  c. 550 BCE

Dedicated to the Greek goddess of the hunt, this temple was rebuilt at least three times on the same site after floods and deliberate destruction — most infamously in 356 BCE, when a man named Herostratus burned it down purely to make his name famous forever. The Ephesians tried to erase him from history by making it illegal to even speak his name, which, ironically, is exactly why we still know it today.

Rebuilt again after the fire into an even grander version, the temple was finally destroyed for good in 268 CE during a Gothic raid. Today, only a single reconstructed column stands on the site, assembled from scattered fragments.

REBUILT
At least 3 times
FINAL DESTRUCTION
268 CE, Gothic raid
STATUS TODAY
One column remains
05 / 07 DESTROYED

The Mausoleum at Halicarnassus

📍 Modern Bodrum, Turkey  ·  c. 350 BCE

Built as a tomb for Mausolus, a regional ruler under the Persian Empire, this monument was so famous for its scale and beauty that his name is the literal origin of the word "mausoleum," still used today for any grand tomb. It combined Greek, Egyptian, and Near Eastern architectural styles into roughly 45 meters of stacked platform, columns, and a stepped pyramid roof, topped with a massive marble chariot sculpture.

A series of earthquakes between the 12th and 15th centuries CE gradually toppled the structure, and by the 1400s, Crusader knights were literally recycling its stone blocks to build a nearby castle — some of which are still visible built into those castle walls today.

HEIGHT
~45 m
LEGACY
Origin of the word "mausoleum"
STATUS TODAY
Ruins, some stone reused in Bodrum Castle
06 / 07 DESTROYED

The Colossus of Rhodes

📍 Rhodes, Greece  ·  c. 280 BCE

A roughly 33-meter bronze statue of the sun god Helios, built to celebrate the island of Rhodes successfully defending itself against a siege. Contrary to the popular image, historians are confident it never straddled the harbor entrance with ships sailing between its legs — the engineering simply wouldn't have supported that pose. It most likely stood on a single point overlooking the harbor, similar in general concept to the Statue of Liberty centuries later.

It stood for only about 54 years before an earthquake in 226 BCE snapped it at the knees. The remains lay on the ground for over 800 years — reportedly still an impressive sight to visitors — until they were eventually sold for scrap metal in the 7th century CE.

07 / 07 DESTROYED

The Lighthouse of Alexandria

📍 Alexandria, Egypt  ·  c. 280 BCE

Built on the island of Pharos to guide ships into Alexandria's busy harbor, this lighthouse stood an estimated 100–130 meters tall, making it one of the tallest structures on Earth for centuries, using a fire beacon at night and possibly a polished bronze mirror to reflect sunlight by day. Its name gave us the modern word "Pharos" or "faro," still used for "lighthouse" in several Romance languages.

A series of earthquakes between the 10th and 14th centuries gradually weakened and finally toppled it. In 2016, Egyptian authorities announced plans to build an underwater museum near the site, where archaeologists have identified large stone blocks believed to be fragments of the original structure, still lying on the harbor floor.

HEIGHT
~100–130 m
LEGACY
Origin of "Pharos / faro"
STATUS TODAY
Ruins on the harbor floor
Of the seven, six were destroyed by exactly the same three forces: earthquakes, fire, and time. Only the Pyramid — built the most simply, from solid stone, with nothing to burn and nothing to topple — made it all the way to today.
